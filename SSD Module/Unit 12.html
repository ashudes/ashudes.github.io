I disagree – the debate is not necessarily yet over. The choice between a monolithic kernel and a microkernel (or other types of kernels) often depends on the specific needs and context of the system in question (ieeexplore.ieee.org, n.d.).

  • Performance: Monolithic kernels often exhibit higher performance, with faster system call execution. This is because all the operating system services run in the same memory space, reducing the overhead of inter-process communication (Biggs et al., 2018).
  • Simplicity: The design of monolithic kernels is simpler to understand and implement, as all components are in a single executable. This can make them easier to maintain and debug (Biggs et al., 2018).
  • Maturity and Stability: Many monolithic kernels, like Linux and Unix, have been around for decades, proving their stability and reliability.

However, it’s important to note that monolithic kernels also have their drawbacks. For instance, if one service fails, it can lead to an entire system failure. Also, adding a new service may require modifying the entire operating system.

In conclusion, I believe the choice between a monolithic kernel and a microkernel still depends on the specific needs. The debate can only be over if both sides come to an agreement that we need both in different circumstances.
